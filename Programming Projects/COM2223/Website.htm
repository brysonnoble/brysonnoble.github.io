Comparative Analysis of ADHD

Demographic Breakdown of ADHD Diagnosis

Around 9% of all children have ADHD (Danielson et al., 2018). Rosenthal et al. (2024) aim to quantify a differential item on functioning ADHD to assess the demographic breakdown of children with ADHD. Factors that are tested include age, gender, and cultural norms that parents or teachers assume. This study utilized a consistent questionnaire, and the setting in which the participants are tested remains constant. Trends that were found lead to indications that male children are generally rated as having more ADHD symptoms than female children. Younger children tend to have higher levels of ADHD than older children, and patterns reflect teachers rating black children as having a higher level of ADHD than white children. An estimated 6.0% of U.S. adults have a current diagnosis of ADHD, totaling approximately 15.5 million individuals (General Prevalence, [n.d.]). Among children ranging from 5–17 years, non-Hispanic white children have a higher prevalence of 13.4% compared to non-Hispanic black 10.8% and Hispanic children of 8.9% are diagnosed with ADHD (CDC, 2024). The statistics suggest that cultural differences arise at different levels of diagnoses across various demographics. However, a large number of the population has been diagnosed with ADHD.
